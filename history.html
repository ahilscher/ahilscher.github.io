1835–1836

THE TEXAS REVOLUTION

No other relay race in America traces its route along actual history. When you run the TIR, you're covering the same ground the Texian settlers fled across in 1836. This is the story of why.

The Story

THE RUNAWAY SCRAPE

Sam Houston could not have predicted the events leading up to March 2, 1836 — the day the Texas Declaration of Independence was signed and Houston was named Commander-in-Chief — nor could he have anticipated everything that would follow before the revolution's end.

The revolution had started five months earlier, on October 2, 1835, in a town you'll come to know well: Gonzales. Mexican soldiers attempted to retrieve a cannon previously given to the American colonists by the Mexican government. The colonists, already showing the spirit of independence that would define Texas, pointed at the small cannon behind them and replied:

COME AND TAKE IT.

A skirmish broke out, sparking the revolution and the long road to Texas independence. A week later, General Martin Perfecto de Cos tried to reestablish Mexican control over the Gulf Coast, but Texian dissenters seized a fort at Goliad. The Battle of Concepcion and the Grass Fight strained relations further. Then, from December 5th to 9th, the Siege of Bexar became the first major engagement — ending in surrender by General Cos and the Mexican cavalry. It also deepened General Santa Anna's conviction to drive the settlers out of Texas entirely.

While Houston was signing the Declaration of Independence at Washington-on-the-Brazos, Colonel James Bowie, Lt. Colonel William Barrett Travis, and Davy Crockett led over 200 Texians in their last stand at the Alamo. They were outnumbered by a ratio greater than 10 to 1, yet they held the fort for 13 days until the siege ended on March 6, 1836. All the Texian fighters were killed. The surviving women and children were told to spread word to the settlers: get out of Texas.

MARCH 13, 1836General Houston ordered everyone to leave their homes and belongings and burn them all, so the Mexican army couldn't benefit from anything left behind. The settlers fled eastward. Their flight became known as the Runaway Scrape.

Two weeks later, Santa Anna ordered the execution of 345 men at Goliad. Similar outcomes occurred at Refugio and Coleto. In all, 400 were executed.

APRIL 21, 1836General Houston and his modestly sized volunteer army surprised and defeated the Mexican army at the Battle of San Jacinto, amid cries of "Remember the Alamo!" and "Remember Goliad!" The battle lasted eighteen minutes. Texas was free.

It's been said that in order to see ahead, you must first look back from where you came. If that's true, then Texas has a bright future — because it was not so long ago that many people exhibited extraordinary courage in claiming Texas' independence.

Where History Meets the Road

YOU RUN THIS GROUND

The TIR route follows the path of the Runaway Scrape. Here's what happened along the road you'll be running.

The Start · Mile 0

GONZALES

Where the first shot of the Texas Revolution was fired. October 2, 1835. The colonists fashioned a flag from a wedding dress — a black star, a cannon, and the words "Come and Take It." Your team starts here, with a cannon boom of your own.

Leg 2 · Mile 10

SAM HOUSTON'S OAK

Leg 2 finishes at the actual oak tree where General Sam Houston briefly made camp in the same month of 1836. Exchange Point 2 sits a stone's throw from where the commander planned his retreat. Teams exchange wristbands in the shadow of the tree.

Legs 3–7 · Miles 10–31

SHINER & THE COUNTRYSIDE

The settlers fled along these same dirt roads. In 1836, this was raw frontier — no towns, no fences, just open prairie. The families moved on foot through here, carrying what they could, burning what they couldn't. Today you'll run past wildflowers and rolling hills on some of the most peaceful roads in the state.

Legs 10–11 · Miles 45–50

FLATONIA & THE PAINTED CHURCHES

The Czech and German immigrants who settled this area after the revolution built churches that are still standing — including St. Mary's in Praha, where Leg 11 finishes. These communities were built by people who came to Texas because of the independence the Texians won.

Legs 14–17 · Miles 62–86

SCHULENBURG → WEIMAR → COLUMBUS

The Runaway Scrape funneled through this corridor. Settlers crossed the Colorado River near Columbus — the same bridge your runners will cross on Leg 21, right around sunset. Columbus was a strategic point in 1836; Houston's army briefly held position here before continuing the retreat east.

Legs 19–23 · Miles 96–118

THE COLORADO RIVER CROSSING

The Colorado River was a significant barrier for the fleeing settlers. Many waited for days to cross with their families and livestock. When your team reaches the Colorado River Bridge on Leg 21, the sun will be setting. The settlers crossed here with fear behind them and uncertainty ahead.

Legs 24–28 · Miles 118–143

THE NIGHT ROAD: EAGLE LAKE → WALLIS

This stretch was the deep interior of the Scrape — families moving through the night, not knowing if the Mexican army was behind them. When you run these legs in the dark, through towns that are sound asleep, you'll have a small sense of what that isolation felt like. The silence out here is something else.

The Finish · Mile ~200

BUFFALO BAYOU & SAN JACINTO

The TIR finishes along Buffalo Bayou — the same waterway where Houston's army camped before the Battle of San Jacinto. On April 21, 1836, just a few miles from where you'll cross the finish line, a ragged volunteer army won Texas its independence in eighteen minutes. Your final stretch runs through Hermann Park, under Sam Houston's statue, and into Saint Arnold Brewery.

The Symbol

THE COME AND TAKE IT FLAG

★
COME AND TAKE IT

Almost instantly after Colonel John Henry Moore uttered the challenge "Come and take it," the phrase became a rallying cry of the Texas Revolution. The flag — originally fashioned from a wedding dress, featuring a black star, a cannon, and the defiant motto — still represents the Texas spirit of independence today.

It's the reason every TIR team starts with a cannon boom. It's the reason the race begins in Gonzales. And it's the reason, after 200 miles and 36 legs, your team runs the final stretch together into a brewery in Houston — because that's what Texans do when they finish something worth finishing.
